Presentation Notes

Identification and Assessment

Mental health professionals:

  • Many of the people involved in the identification of emotional and behavioral disabilities are medical professionals, not educators.
  • Some examples of these medical professionals are psychologists, psychiatrists, and nurse practitioners.
  • The school psychologist is an example of a school staff member who has the relevant expertise.

Formal assessments:

  • List of formal assessments that are used to determine appropriate services
  • SAED-3: norm-referenced, meaning results are compared to each other, and closely aligns with the different aspects of the federal definition
  • BASC-3 and BRP-2: rating systems that survey parents, educators, and sometimes students
    • The rating scales cover a wide variety of emotions and behaviors, and also track trends such as mostly positive, mostly negative, or inconsistent responses.
  • WISC-V: is cognitive disability causing the behavior?
  • Tests of Achievement: how much the emotional & behavior disorders are affecting educational performance

Informal observations:

  • Formal assessments are only one dimension
  • In order to get the full picture, professionals can:
    • Observe the student in their classroom
    • Review recent examples of schoolwork
    • Consider their family history, and interview the student and their family members
    • Examine medical records
    • Focus on strengths rather than weaknesses.

Criteria for eligibility:

  • To be eligible for special education under IDEA, the student must first display at least one of the characteristics in the federal definition:
    • Strange trouble learning
    • Lack of social relationships
    • Unexpected emotional reactions
    • Frequent sadness
    • Physical symptoms of emotions
  • Then, the characteristics of the student must have a negative impact on educational performance
  • Finally, the federal definition does not include social maladjustment
    • However, this is heavily contested by many professional organizations
    • Some states rewrite or omit this requirement completely

Instructional Techniques and Accommodations

Early childhood:

  • Since behaviors are learned, teaching positive behaviors from the beginning can make a big difference
  • Even before birth, new parents can prepare for the challenges of childcare
  • Once the child is in a preschool program, modelling positive behavior is important, even before any misbehavior happens
  • Providing positive feedback for positive behavior and specific, constructive feedback for negative behavior reinforces development
  • Finally, teaming with parents can extend the development of positive behavior from the classroom to the home

Inclusion:

  • General education classrooms can present new challenges for growing students
  • Curricula rarely address social & emotional needs, and can simultaneously create pressure with high expectations
  • In the classroom environment, students can struggle to build meaningful relationships
  • Because of classroom limitations, mental health counseling cannot reasonably be incorporated

General educator support:

  • To mitigate these challenges, general educators can implement supports to help their students as much as possible.
  • For example, choose books for class reading assignments so that the characters can model positive social skills
  • Class discussions can have a foundation in courteous language
  • Teachers and students can create behavior contracts to specifically list positive rewards and negative consequences.
  • In schools with a wide age range or school systems that collaborate, students can be partnered with older role models in mentorship programs.

Functional behavior assessment:

  • The IDEA requires two documents to support students with emotional & behavior disabilities: the Functional Behavior Assessment and the Behavior Intervention Plan
  • The Functional Behavior Assessment is used to find root causes for behaviors under the assumption that these behaviors have purpose
  • To complete the FBA:
    • First, quantify the occurrences of behavior: "A student blurted out an answer in class 3 times instead of raising their hand"
    • Then, describe the setting of the behavior: "This happened during English class and Science class"
    • Collect more information about the behavior:
      • What came immediately before and after the behavior?
      • For how long did the behavior occur?
    • Review all information, and gather additional perspectives from other educators as well as the student and their family
    • Finally, form a hypothesis about why the behavior happens.
  • This hypothesis will be the basis of the Behavior Intervention Plan
    • Now that the cause of the behavior has been determined, the BIP outlines strategies to change the behavior
