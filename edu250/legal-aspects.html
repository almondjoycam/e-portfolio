Legal Aspects and Critical Issues in Special Education

Foundations of Special Education

There are many facets to special education, shaped by different concepts and legislation.

Special Education

Specially designed instruction to meet the unique needs of a child with a disability (Friend 4).

Related Services

Support services, such as transportation, interpreting, counseling, and therapy, enabling students to receive a free and appropriate public education (Friend 4).

Not directly related to instruction, but allow the education to be accessed.

Supplementary Aids and Services

Aids, services, and other supports that are provided in regular education classes to access the general curriculum (Friend 5).

Assistive technology and preferential seating fall into this category.

Early Special Education Laws

The Elementary and Secondary Education Act of 1965

P.L. 89-750

The Federal Government now provides financial assistance to states to create and improve special education programs (Friend 10).

The Education of the Handicapped Act of 1975

P.L. 94-142

This law contained the first definitions of many special education concepts that were being argued in the courts.

It also mandated that states follow the law in order to be eligible for federal funding (Friend 10).

1986 Reauthorization of the EHA

This reauthorization of the Education of the Handicapped Act added infant and early childhood care and intervention (Friend 10).

Previously, services were unavailable until children reached the age of three.

The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act of 1990 (IDEA)

The reauthorization of the EHA in 1990 gave the law a new name as well.

This added the need for transition planning as students transition between schools as well as to college or into the workforce (Friend 10).

IDEA Reauthorizations

In 1997, the law was expanded to clarify methods of discipline, parental involvement, and the role of general education teachers.

In 2004, the most recent revision added more strategies to resolve disputes between parents and educators (Friend 10).

IDEA Pillar 1: Zero Reject

All disabled students are entitled to a public education, no matter what.

States have programs in place to inform the public and screen students for potential disabilities.

Ensures that students with communicable diseases or disciplinary records are educated as well (Friend 12).

IDEA Pillar 2: Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE)

Parents and family members must not be asked to pay for special education services.

Students with disabilities must receive a plan to specialize their instruction appropriately for their needs (Friend 12).

IDEA Pillar 3: Least Restrictive Environment (LRE)

Separating disabled students from their peers must be appropriately justified.

Every effort must be made to keep disabled students in the same environment as their peers (Friend 12).

IDEA Pillar 4: Non-Discriminatory Evaluation

Tests are unbiased, administered in the child's native language, and appropriate for the child.

More than one test is used to assess the presence of a disability, and all areas of suspected disability are assessed.

All tests are administered and interpreted by knowledgeable professionals (Friend 12).

IDEA Pillar 5: Confidentiality

Information regarding student disabilities is confidential, and everyone who accesses this information should be recorded.

Parents have a right to request access to all records regarding their child, and can dispute potential inaccuracies (Friend 13).

IDEA Pillar 6: Procedural Safeguards

Parents must give consent for their child to be tested, be invited to any meetings regarding their child, and give permission for their child to receive special education services (Friend 13).

Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973

This law prohibits discrimination based on disability in programs receiving federal funding.

It mandates equal access to education and other public services by providing reasonable accommodations, and covers a wider range of disabilities, as well as adults (Friend 13).

Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990

ADA protects the rights of all individuals with disabilities across both public and private sectors (Friend 14).

Accessible entrances to buildings are mandated through ADA.

Inclusion and Collaboration

In an inclusive education environment, all students can access learning in the way that works best for them.

Research has shown that students with disabilities learn and perform better when they are included with their peers.

General and special education teachers must work together to teach all their students (Friend 21-24).

The Role of the Parents

Parents can be their child's biggest advocate in education, because they know their child's strengths better than most.

However, finding time for parents to collaborate with educators can be difficult depending on many social factors (Friend 18-19).

The Essence of Special Education

All students should have a right to education, and many laws in recent history ensure this.

In order to be the most effective, special education involves many different people collaborating to best accommodate each student.

Works Cited
