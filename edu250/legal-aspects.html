EDU 250

What defines special education?

Special Education

Specially designed instruction to meet the unique needs of a child with a disability.

Related services

Support services, such as transportation, interpreting, counseling, and therapy, enabling students to receive a free and appropriate public education. Not directly related to instruction, but allow the education to be accessed.

Supplementary aids and services

Aids, services, and other supports that are provided in regular education classes Examples: assistive technology, preferential seating

Early Special Education Laws

The Elementary and Secondary Education Act of 1965

P.L. 89-750

Provided funding to states to create and improve special education programs

The Education of the Handicapped Act of 1975

P.L. 94-142

Amendments to the ESEA The basis for all subsequent special education practice Mandate that states follow the law in order to receive federal funding First defined FAPE, rights of students and their parents, provide federal funding assistance, and assessment / accountability practices

1986 Reauthorization of the EHA

Include infant and early childhood care and intervention Previously, services were unavailable until children reached the age of 3

The Individuals with Disabilities Education Act of 1990 (IDEA)

The current name of the law, a reauthorization of EHA Added traumatic brain injury and autism as IDEA categories Mandated transition planning between schools as well as to the workforce or to college In VA, children can begin attending their IEP meetings at age 14 or in 8th grade (whichever comes first) Access to general curriculum is emphasized Developmental Delay category expanded, possibly to age 9 Included ways for parents to resolve disputes

IDEA Reauthorizations

Added to in 1997 to clarify discipline, parental involvement, and role of general education teachers Revised in 2004 to add more strategies to resolve disputes with parents

IDEA Pillar 1: Zero Reject

All disabled students are entitled to a public education no matter what States have "child find" programs to inform the public and screen students for potential disability Also applies to students with communicable diseases and disabled students who may have committed disciplinary offenses

IDEA Pillar 2: Free Appropriate Public Education (FAPE)

Parents and family members must not be asked to pay for special education services Students must have an IEP to specialize their instruction appropriately for their needs

IDEA Pillar 3: Least Restrictive Environment (LRE)

Separating disabled students from their peers must be appropriately justified Every effort must be made to keep disabled students in the same environment as their peers

IDEA Pillar 4: Non-Discriminatory Evaluation

Any assessment that is part of a special education screening or decision process must be unbiased Tests are administered in the child's native language and are appropriate for the child's age and characters More than one test is used to assess the presence of a disability, and assessments occur in all areas of suspected disability Tests are administered and interpreted by a knowledgeable professional

IDEA Pillar 5: Confidentiality

Confidential information regarding student disabilities should only be shared with the individuals working with the student A log must be kept of anyone who accesses student records Parents have a right to request access to all records regarding their child, and can dispute potential inaccuracies

IDEA Pillar 6: Procedural Safeguards

Parents must give consent for their child to be tested Parents must be invited to any meetings regarding their child Parents must give permission for their child to receive special education services

Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act of 1973

Prohibits discrimination based on disability in programs receiving federal funding Ensures equal access to education and other public services Provides reasonable accommodations Covers a wider range of disabilities as well as adults

Americans with Disabilities Act of 1990

Protects the rights of individuals with disabilities Applies to both public and private sectors Accessibility in buildings Covers employment accommodations
