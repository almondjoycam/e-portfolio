IEP Meeting Observations

A Sample IEP Meeting

This video from the Exceptional Children's Assistance Center shows some of the highlights of an IEP meeting. The multidisciplinary team that participates in IEP decision making requires the presence of many people, including those listed below.

  • Special Education Teacher
  • Notetaker
  • General Education Teacher
  • Parents
  • Student
  • School Psychologist
  • Middle School Representative
  • Assistant Principal

The special education teacher took on the role of the team leader, who presented all the different parts of the IEP plan to the rest of the participants. The notetaker recorded detailed notes of everything discussed during the meeting. The general education teacher provided both quantitative and qualitative evidence of how the student performed in their class. The parents and the student each provided their own anecdotal evidence of how the student was performing both in and out of school. The school psychologist was able to interpret results of any diagnostic testing that the student had undergone. The middle school representative was able to plan the student's transition from elementary to middle school. Finally, the assistant principal represented the school district at the meeting.

Student performance data were used to influence team decisions primarily when they discussed reducing the time that the student spent in a separate special education setting. Both the parents and the assistant principal mentioned the student's below average performance in classes to argue against this time reduction, and the team's ultimate decision was to leave this time unchanged.

The student receives 45 minutes of separate special education a week as one of their services. As an accommodation, the student also is given the opportunity to complete written work on the computer, instead of writing it out by hand.

When the team was discussing the student's accommodation to use a computer for written assignments, I noticed that the parents seemed very dismissive of assistive technology, asking, “Why would my child need assistive technology?” However, this question may also have stemmed from a place of curiosity, since the parents likely had a very limited understanding of the full extent of assistive technology. The general educator's response to the parents' question seemed unsatisfactory to me as well, but the parents seemed to understand better. From this, it seems that I, a disabled adult, understand disability vastly differently than how parents understand their children with disabilities.

The student left shortly after their IEP meeting began because they had to return to class. I found it surprising both that the student was not invited to stay for their entire meeting, and that these meetings occurred during school hours. Although it makes sense to the multidisciplinary team members to meet during their common working hours, the parents likely had to miss a partial or full day of work to attend the meeting, especially since two parents were present. In particular, when the team was discussing the student's annual goal, the student might have been able to offer valuable feedback on whether they themself thought that the goal was attainable.

When the parents asked to see an example of a graphic organizer, the educators were able to pull one out to show immediately. From this, I learned that having recent examples of student work is important to bring to an IEP meeting. I was surprised that the educators were able to provide this example, because I definitely could not have predicted that the parents would ask about that specific tool.
