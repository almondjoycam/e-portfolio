Universal Design for Learning Application Project

EDU 250

UDL Lesson Plan

Goal

The student will use iterative design process to create a program. Identify the goal and objectives of the program. (MSCSE-9.AP.2a)

(Grades 7-8)

Lesson Description

The teacher will open the class demonstrating the process of designing a computer program to the entire group. Students will spend time brainstorming ideas about a computer program or game to create, and then fill out the first page of a game design document template. Students will then practice further refining their ideas. At the end of the class, students are given the opportunity to share their ideas with the class.

Multiple Means of Engagement

Recruiting Students' Interests
  • Students will have the opportunity to choose the theme and type of their program or game.
  • During the brainstorming phase, students will have the option to work in groups, with the teacher, or independently.
  • The design template for their program will be available in both paper and digital formats.
Sustaining Effort and Persistence
  • This lesson is solely dedicated to the brainstorming and planning phase, so as to not overwhelm students with the task of designing a program.
  • The teacher will encourage the students' creativity, but provide gentle reminders to keep it simple in scope.
  • If students finish early, they are encouraged to begin working on a prototype.
Options for Self-Regulation
  • If students are overwhelmed and need a break, they can take a design template home, or work on the template in the hallway.
  • Students can change their group preference if a current arrangement isn't working well.

Multiple Means of Representation

Touch, Sight, and Sound
  • At a central table or desk, students will have access to a board game that they can use as an example for game and program design.
  • The graphic organizer template for designing the game or program will have areas for students to either write or draw their responses.
  • Students will have access to a video that explains game and program design to watch on their devices.
Language and Symbols
  • Before beginning the lesson, review and define terminology such as “program” and “design.”
  • Use symbols such as a flag for the goal of a game.
Options for Comprehension
  • Ask students about their favorite games that they play, and break down the game's design as a class.
  • Provide different worksheets with different reading and planning complexity. A simpler template could be a 5 W's, while a more complex template could include prompts relating to the story, background, or obstacles.

Multiple Means of Action & Expression

Active Learning & Communicating Knowledge
  • At the end of the class, students can choose to share the goal of their game aloud with the class.
  • Students can also choose to write or draw their responses on the graphic organizer.
  • An example of a gesture for a game's objective could be “moving from here (left hand) to there (right hand).”
Scaffolding Responses
  • A graphic organizer can serve as a scaffold.
  • Some sentence frames that could prompt student responses might include “To win the game, you have to…” or “The goal of the game is…”.
  • Students can write out a few of their interests, and then circle a topic for their game based on their list.
Executive Function
  • The teacher can model how to choose a topic and then develop a goal for a game based on the topic.
  • Students will have a rubric that they can use to self-assess their responses.
  • After coming up with an objective for the game, the teacher will conference with students individually to reflect on and minimize the planned scope.
