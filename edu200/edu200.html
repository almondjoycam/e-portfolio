Philosophy of Education

How do students learn?

Students learn through examples that are tangible and relevant to their own lives. They also learn through experiencing concepts for themselves, instead of in a isolated environment that is disconnected from the outside world. Students are members of society, both now and in the future. They need to be able to choose how to integrate themselves further into this society as time progresses. Students cannot learn from just one person, for there is a massive expanse of knowledge in the world. One individual cannot possibly be an expert in all fields. Instead, each student fulfills their own knowledge niche.

What should students be taught?

Students should start by learning a little bit of everything. Maths and sciences are just as valued as arts and humanities, and an equal emphasis is placed on learning from all cultures and societies. The core foundation in all subjects should provide a starting point for students to begin learning. I'm not the sole example of what a functional adult should know, and so the variety of subjects to teach should reflect many more possibilities. The students then can use this foundational knowledge to build themselves in the direction of their interests.

Under what conditions do students learn the best?

Students learn best in a safe space, where they can be comfortable bringing their own experiences as examples to the classroom. A community effort to solve problems in students' lives can be a very effective teaching method. If a student feels inadequately represented in the classroom, they should advocate for themselves and their cultures to be recognised, understood, and celebrated in the classroom as well. Similarly, the interests of each student should be equally valued and celebrated as well. Students should not be pushed to learn certain disciplines, nor should their interests in disciplines be suppressed in any way.

How should students be taught?

Everything is connected, and no subject is an island. Concrete examples from real life can be used to teach many different lessons across subject matters. For example, building a bridge involves engineering it to not break, calculating the maximum load it can hold, choosing materials that are both aesthetically pleasing and sturdy, studying bridges from history that have both failed and been successful, and using different terminology to describe the process to many different audiences. Bridges also have a lot of figurative and social symbolism! How do actual bridges represent symbolic bridges, and vice versa?

What qualities comprise a good teacher?

The teacher should relate concepts to students in terms of tangible examples. Teachers come with their own unique real-life experiences that can be shared to better inform students. The teacher should discuss their own knowledge niche, and direct students to appropriate resources for other knowledge niches. The teacher should create a safe space in the classroom where the students are free to express themselves and their troubles. Learning occurs best when the environment promotes a state of safety and comfort.

Communicating with Parents

Email threads provide a critical written record of communications that other media cannot maintain. Mailing lists can broadcast information to an entire group — for example, all parents of students in a certain class — but can also remain personal between a teacher and a parent if necessary. Most modern email supports rich text formatting as well, which can provide additional emphasis, and composing the written word affords an opportunity for revision.

Many opportunities for in-person communication are already built in to the school year. Parent-teacher conferences give parents a chance to influence the learning experience for their children. Open house events introduce the parents to the team responsible for their child's learning. Teachers can use these opportunities to expand upon their teaching philosophy and class expectations.

Parents will very likely be able to access a form of instant messaging with their children's teachers. This can be a necessary form of communication for asking quick questions and providing quick notes. This can also allow parents to engage more in their child's learning experience.

Students take home material from school nearly every day, and this constant exchange of information is critical to family involvement in education. Surveys can be an opportunity for the student to assign homework to their parent, who can then inform the teacher directly about how their child learns and behaves. Sending home completed work can encourage students to proudly share their work with their family as well.

Phone calls are generally the best way to reach someone instantly. These can be used to resolve urgent matters, but should also be used to communicate positive news. In fact, positive news should precede any concerns or requests.

Class newsletters can be useful for providing updates about the class activities. Like any communication, it is important to maintain honesty, transparency, and respect. Everything, including both the good news and the bad news, is of equal importance.

Instructional Strategies

From my own experiences with learning, I find education much more interactive and memorable when each of the 5 senses is involved.

Instruction by sight involves a student using both their sense of sight and their capacity to visualise. A passive example of sight instruction would be reading an article about a given topic, where the words can be seen and re-read if needed. An active example of sight instruction would be creating a map or chart to help other students visualise the topic.

Instruction by smell, to me, represents "sniffing out" a topic in an investigative way. Research projects are one of my personal favourite strategies to investigate a topic. They give students an opportunity to not only learn about concepts in their own way, but also to connect a topic with their own interests and knowledge.

Instruction by taste is experienced when I can feel, and almost taste, the form of words in my mouth as I speak. Students can experience this by reading aloud textual content about their topic. They can also apply their knowledge further in a creative way by presenting about topics in order to teach each other.

Instruction by touch can incorporate a personal element to learning a topic. Hands-on activities allow students to experience content with their own hands. In a similar vein, role-playing activities can lead students to connect themselves with the topic, and playing games creates an environment that encourages active learning.

Instruction by audio occurs when students listen to content related to a topic. This can be commonly seen in a lecture format, which I have found to be ineffective when it's the only form of instruction. However, when combined with other senses, it can be a lot more engaging. Listening to songs about the topic is also a very helpful way to become familiar with information; sometimes I still wake up with the Preamble to the U.S. Constitution or the Periodic Table of the Elements stuck in my head.

Technology in the Classroom

Games

I have heard many students self-describe as being unable to learn and focus, yet I've seen these same students spend hours focusing on a video game, learning the game's strategy and lore. To me, this means video games are becoming better teachers than schools, and so educators must take notes. To succeed in a game often requires a lot of trial and error, but the crucial difference between games and school is that a game's response to failure is to simply respawn and try again. I found the article How to Successfully Gamify a Course to be an excellent resources of some empirically-determined strategies for learning gamification.

Privacy

A new technology resource I see appearing in schools is some form of surveillance software, where teachers are able to monitor and even control the content on student devices. As both an educator and a technology professional, this highly disturbs me. Not only does this level of control decrease trust between students and teachers, but these sofwares are in possession of a vast amount of data about these students. Since these softwares are often proprietary and licensed, the companies providing them are looking to make a profit, and I know that data is a valuable commodity in the tech industry. I don't want to knowingly compromise student privacy.

Accessibility

One of the most significant benefits of technology in the classroom is increasing the accessibility of course content. The ability to convert text to speech, speech to text, translate languages, and countless other assistive technology features are now available to the majority of students. This means that students who might not necessarily receive specific accommodations can still access content in alternative formats. Since accommodations are often reliant on professional medical diagnoses, which have a significant cost barrier as well as social impact, some students who may need them might not receive them. Making assistive technologies available to all students can help close this gap.

Collaboration

One of the most important skills learned in school is socialisation. Students learn to interact with one another in both formal and informal environments by simply being in a classroom with others. Technology can foster this collaboration both in the classroom and beyond by allowing multiple students to build on the same digital project. This can even lead into global collaborations, where students can be exposed to many different cultures other than their own.

Digital Literacy

Since technology facilitates a massive exchange of information, it's only expected that there are inaccuracies in resources. Using technology in the classroom mandates a critical approach to all digital resources. Digital articles can contain false information, and there are websites whose authors may not possess any relevant credentials. Additionally, the fair use of content is important to learn, to ensure that appropriate credit is given and all used content has the proper permissions. Generative AI that trains on data from the Internet can contain false information, licensed or otherwise personal work, and content for which creators have not given machine-learning usage permissions. As a result, it is crucial to approach digital resources in the classroom while being mindful of fair use, false information, and digital courtesy.

Assessment

Entry and exit tickets are very short formative assessments, often containing less than five questions, that provide a quick glimpse of how well students are learning the content on a particular day. Because they are so short, they often do not cause the same testing anxiety that other, more formal assessments may create. Their brevity can also be detrimental, though, because if the lesson was particularly complicated, it can be hard to condense the content accurately.

Projects are a form of authentic assessment that allow students to have more freedom and flexibility in demonstrating their content mastery. Some projects that I've experienced as a student have also allowed me to connect new concepts with prior learning and my own personal interests. They can be very difficult for students who may not have strong creative visions or prefer direct instruction, in which case scaffolding, such as templates, may be necessary.

Classwork and homework assignments give students an opportunity to practice applying concepts that they learn during class. Without practicing, it can be a lot more difficult to learn and master content. It is important to be conservative with this approach; otherwise, an excess of homework can cause school to bleed in to a student's personal life, health, and responsibilities, and produce a negative effect where it is harder for the students to learn in class.

Self-assessments allow the students to reflect on their own mastery, quality of work, and learning standards. Self-assessments include the student in a more active role in determining their grade, and can also provide insights on the student's social-emotional self-image. Because the student has more power in determining their grade in self-assessment, though, they may overestimate or underestimate their own potential.

Formal, summative assessments are the tests administered at the end of a unit or term. These are important to gauge how much students have learned over the course of the time period. However, a lot of formal testing environments induce stress in students, and may impact their performance on the assessment to a point where this assessment may not be an accurate reflection of content mastery.
