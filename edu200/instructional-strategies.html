Instructional Strategies

From my own experiences with learning, I find education much more interactive and memorable when each of the 5 senses is involved.

Instruction by sight involves a student using both their sense of sight and their capacity to visualise. A passive example of sight instruction would be reading an article about a given topic, where the words can be seen and re-read if needed. An active example of sight instruction would be creating a map or chart to help other students visualise the topic.

Instruction by smell, to me, represents "sniffing out" a topic in an investigative way. Research projects are one of my personal favourite strategies to investigate a topic. They give students an opportunity to not only learn about concepts in their own way, but also to connect a topic with their own interests and knowledge.

Instruction by taste is experienced when I can feel, and almost taste, the form of words in my mouth as I speak. Students can experience this by reading aloud textual content about their topic. They can also apply their knowledge further in a creative way by presenting about topics in order to teach each other.

Instruction by touch can incorporate a personal element to learning a topic. Hands-on activities allow students to experience content with their own hands. In a similar vein, role-playing activities can lead students to connect themselves with the topic, and playing games creates an environment that encourages active learning.

Instruction by sound occurs when students listen to content related to a topic. This can be commonly seen in a lecture format, which I have found to be ineffective when it's the only form of instruction. However, when combined with other senses, it can be a lot more engaging. Listening to songs about the topic is also a very helpful way to become familiar with information; sometimes I still wake up with the Preamble to the U.S. Constitution or the Periodic Table of the Elements stuck in my head.
