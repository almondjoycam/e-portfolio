Assessment

Entry and exit tickets are very short formative assessments, often containing less than five questions, that provide a quick glimpse of how well students are learning the content on a particular day. Because they are so short, they often do not cause the same testing anxiety that other, more formal assessments may create. Their brevity can also be detrimental, though, because if the lesson was particularly complicated, it can be hard to condense the content accurately.

Projects are a form of authentic assessment that allow students to have more freedom and flexibility in demonstrating their content mastery. Some projects that I've experienced as a student have also allowed me to connect new concepts with prior learning and my own personal interests. They can be very difficult for students who may not have strong creative visions or prefer direct instruction, in which case scaffolding, such as templates, may be necessary.

Classwork and homework assignments give students an opportunity to practice applying concepts that they learn during class. Without practicing, it can be a lot more difficult to learn and master content. It is important to be conservative with this approach; otherwise, an excess of homework can cause school to bleed in to a student's personal life, health, and responsibilities, and produce a negative effect where it is harder for the students to learn in class.

Self-assessments allow the students to reflect on their own mastery, quality of work, and learning standards. Self-assessments include the student in a more active role in determining their grade, and can also provide insights on the student's social-emotional self-image. Because the student has more power in determining their grade in self-assessment, though, they may overestimate or underestimate their own potential.

Formal, summative assessments are the tests administered at the end of a unit or term. These are important to gauge how much students have learned over the course of the time period. However, a lot of formal testing environments induce stress in students, and may impact their performance on the assessment to a point where this assessment may not be an accurate reflection of content mastery.
