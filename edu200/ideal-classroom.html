Ideal Classroom

"Fidget Toys 1" by MissLunaRose12 is licensed under CC BY-SA 4.0 .

This is an image of various fidget toys on a rainbow striped mat.

  • A Koosh ball has many strings of multicoloured rubber in yellow, blue, green, and red.
  • A three-by-three Rubik's cube rests on the mat, solved, with the blue side facing up and the yellow and red sides facing to the side.
  • A plastic tube is filled with a yellow-tinted fluid, and there are metallic sequins shaped like stars amd music notes suspended within.
  • Two rubber puffer balls with spikes, one dark green and the other bright orange, are at either end of the tube.
  • A handheld white object has three ball sockets arranged in a triangular pattern.
  • A loop of bumpy plastic elbow joints in blue, purple, and green has been twisted into a serpentine shape.
  • A white plastic bristle brush is on its side, with a textured handle.

These are examples of the countless fidget toys that exist. My ideal classroom would have a wide variety of items like these to fidget with to meet a variety of students' sensory needs. Providing students a source of non-disruptive sensory input can help them focus better, learn better, and feel better in the classroom.

In this image, two foldable chairs with animal themes are set up next to a table with a blue plastic organiser. This organiser provides a handy way to hold a stereo, three sets of headphones, and an adapter to plug in multiple headphone jacks to the stereo at once.

Students learn best in a multitude of diverse ways, and one way to accommodate alternative learning is to provide a way for students to learn through audio recordings. Containing the various audio resources within headphones can be crucial to maintaining the order in a classroom where many things are going on at the same time.

This reading corner is a bench against a window, with a cushion and pillows. Greenery fills the window box.

Classrooms are often short on space, and so a way to conserve this space would be to set up seating space on top of bookshelves similar to this reading bench. Additionally, natural light and green spaces are very beneficial to learning. Tasking students with watering the plants may even foster a positive sense of responsibility.

In this image, there are many different examples of alternative seating choices to choose from! Two tables are lower to the ground, one with wobble cushions set up and the other with cushioned mats. At least two tables have chairs resembling exercise balls with stabilising legs. Many standard plastic chairs are spread throughout the room. There's also a wobble stool visible at one table!

Flexible seating such as the examples in this picture make the classroom look more colourful and exciting. It can also offer valuable sensory feedback for students who learn better while moving. I might have to put a wobble stool at my own desk.

In this image, rows of desks with chairs and computers face forward. Mathematical diagrams cover not only the white board at the front of the room, but even the walls!

My algebra teacher had coated the walls of her room in dry erasable paint, and a frequent class activity was to solve problems by literally writing on the walls. This class made such an impact on me that I haven't forgotten it since, and I'd like to implement something similar in my own classroom. There's something so transgressively appealing about being able and allowed to write on the walls.
